A GUIDE FOR SOFTWARE ENGINEERS

European structured products — what they are and how they actually work

You invest €100 in a bank-issued financial product. A few months later, SAP shares are up 15%. Depending on which product you chose, you could be up 37%, up 9%, still exactly at €100, or sitting on a near-total loss — even though the underlying share rose the whole time. This guide explains why, using concrete numbers and analogies aimed at engineers rather than traders.

01 — CONCEPT

What is a structured product?

Think of it this way: a bank takes a normal share — say SAP, trading at €200 — and repackages it into something with different risk/reward characteristics. Instead of buying the share directly, you buy a product the bank designed. It might give you leverage, protect your downside, or cap your upside in exchange for a purchase discount. Same underlying share, completely different outcome depending on what happens to the price.

Under the hood, all structured products are just two ingredients combined: a bond (which handles the capital guarantee or provides the financing) and an option (which delivers the market exposure with the specific payoff shape you want). The bank packages these together into a single tradeable security with an ISIN, lists it on an exchange, and quotes live buy/sell prices throughout the trading day.

Crucially, there is no anonymous order book matching retail buyers with other sellers. When you trade a structured product, you always trade directly against the issuing bank. The bank is the market maker for its own products, quoting bid/ask prices continuously. It hedges its exposure in the derivatives market and earns the spread. This means issuer credit risk is always present — if the bank fails, your claim is as an unsecured creditor, even if the underlying SAP share is perfectly fine.

Where do these trade in Europe? Primarily on Börse Stuttgart (EUWAX) and Börse Frankfurt. Stuttgart is Germany's dominant retail structured products venue — EUWAX stands for European Warrant Exchange. Frankfurt lists over 1.8 million structured product ISINs, the largest such market in the world. They are traded through normal brokerage accounts alongside regular stocks and ETFs. The main issuers are Deutsche Bank, DZ Bank, Société Générale, BNP Paribas, HSBC, and Commerzbank. Retail investors in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland treat them as a standard part of their investment toolkit.
02 — PRODUCT TYPES

The four main shapes

Every structured product is a variation on one of these four designs. They represent different tradeoffs between upside participation, downside protection, and leverage. The interactive sections below let you see exactly what each pays — at maturity, and over time.

Participation certificate Simple
Tracks the underlying share 1:1 and is economically identical to owning the share directly. It's issued as bank debt rather than equity, so it adds one risk the share doesn't have: issuer credit risk. If the bank fails, you lose — even if SAP is doing fine. Mainly used when investors can't or don't want to hold shares directly (certain fund mandates, tax wrappers).
Engineer analogy: a proxy object around a stock. No extra logic, just indirection — and now you're depending on the proxy staying alive.
Discount certificate Moderate
You buy the underlying at a 10% discount — €180 instead of €200. In exchange, your gains are capped at a ceiling (€220). You beat the stock in flat or falling markets; you get left behind in a strong rally. The structure is: long stock + short call at the cap. The call option premium the bank collects is passed on to you as the discount.
Engineer analogy: a rate-limited API. Full speed below the cap, hard throttle above it. You traded max throughput for a cheaper subscription tier.
Capital protected note Lower risk
Your €100 is guaranteed back at maturity no matter how far the stock falls. In exchange, you only get 60% of the upside above €200. The mechanism: the bank uses ~85% of your money to buy a zero-coupon bond (guarantees €100 at maturity) and spends the remaining €15 on a call option — which at current volatility only buys 60% participation. The higher the interest rate, the more left over for the option and the better the participation rate.
Engineer analogy: a try/catch with a guaranteed fallback value. The happy path can fail completely, but the function always returns at least 100.
Turbo / knock-out certificate High risk
The bank lends you €160 (the strike) to buy the €200 share — you put in only €40. Your €100 buys 2.5 turbos. Leverage at entry: 5× per turbo (200/40). The barrier at €170 is the bank's circuit breaker: if the share touches €170 at any point — intraday, even momentarily — the product terminates immediately. You receive a small residual: (barrier − strike) × quantity = (170−160) × 2.5 = €25. The share can recover afterwards. It doesn't matter — the turbo is already dead.
Engineer analogy: a process with a watchdog timer and no signal handler. If a threshold is breached, it receives SIGKILL. No cleanup, no restart, no recovery.

05 — KEY LESSONS

What to remember

01 The turbo knock-out is permanent and instantaneous. The barrier is checked continuously — intraday, not just at close. A brief dip that recovers the same afternoon is enough to terminate the product forever. This is why bank risk systems treating barrier monitoring as a real-time, mission-critical function — every tick in the underlying price feed triggers a check against every outstanding barrier level.
02 The discount certificate outperforms in flat or falling markets. You bought at a 10% discount, so you're ahead from day one if the stock stays around €200. The cap only hurts you in strong rallies. Investors who expect a sideways or mildly bullish market pick this over direct stock ownership. Investors who expect a big move don't.
03 Capital protection has a hard cost tied to interest rates. At low rates, most of your €100 goes into the bond (PV of €100 might be €92 if rates are low), leaving only €8 for the option. That buys very little participation — maybe 40%. At higher rates, the bond costs less (say €85), leaving €15 for the option, buying 60% participation. This is why capital-protected products were almost unsellable during the 2010s zero-rate era.
04 All structured products carry issuer credit risk that owning a share directly does not. When you hold SAP shares, you own equity in SAP. When you hold a structured product, you own an unsecured debt obligation of the issuing bank. If Deutsche Bank fails, your claim on the SAP exposure goes into the insolvency queue alongside other creditors — even if SAP itself is at an all-time high.
05 The data engineering challenge is scale, not complexity. Barrier checking is simple logic. But Frankfurt alone lists 1.8 million structured product ISINs. Each has a reference to an underlying, a barrier level, a strike, and a maturity. Every tick in any underlying price triggers lookups against all associated products. Maintaining accurate reference data for 1.8 million instruments — especially across corporate actions like splits and dividends that require mechanical adjustment of barrier and strike levels — is where the operational complexity lives.
