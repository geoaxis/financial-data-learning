A field guide for software engineers

Financial market data — what it is and how it behaves

Seven topics covering the asset classes and data patterns you'll encounter when building on a market data feed. Each section pairs the financial concept with the engineering reality.

01 — Foundation

How market data works

Before looking at any asset class, understand the three dimensions that cut across all of them: what kind of data you're getting, how fresh it is, and how securities are identified. These concepts apply regardless of which API or vendor you use.

Exchange vs OTC Real-time / Delayed / EOD ISIN / SEDOL / MIC Reference vs Quote Data
The two types of market data

Reference Data (static)

Describes what a security is. Changes infrequently — maybe once a year at a corporate action, otherwise stable.

Examples: company name, ISIN, exchange listing, coupon rate, maturity date, fund manager, index constituents.

Engineering pattern: cache aggressively. Invalidate on corporate action events. Treat like a config file, not a stream.

Quote Data (dynamic)

Describes what price a security is trading at right now. Can change thousands of times per second.

Examples: bid, ask, last price, volume, OHLCV bars, yield, NAV.

Engineering pattern: streaming or short-TTL cache. Three freshness levels: real-time (milliseconds), delayed (15–20 min), EOD (once daily at close).
Exchange-traded vs OTC

Exchange-traded

All orders flow through a central venue (NYSE, LSE, Xetra…). Every trade is public — price, volume, time. The exchange is both the matching engine and the price reporter.

Data feed: a firehose of order book events and trade prints. You need to filter by symbol. Latency matters — co-location is a thing.

Examples: equities, listed options, exchange-traded futures, ETFs.

Over the Counter (OTC)

Two parties negotiate directly — no central exchange. Price is agreed bilaterally, which means there is no single official price.

Data feed: aggregated quotes from multiple market makers or inter-dealer brokers. The "price" is a consensus or best available. Transparency is lower.

Examples: bonds, FX spot, interest rate swaps, most structured products.
Security identifiers — the Tower of Babel

Why the same security has many IDs

There is no single global standard everyone uses. Different jurisdictions and vendors invented their own systems:

ISIN — ISO 6166, 12-character global standard. One per security. Use this for cross-border lookups.
SEDOL — 7 characters, UK/Ireland. Common in European systems.
CUSIP — 9 characters, North America.
VALOR — Switzerland.
WKN — Germany (6 characters).
MIC — ISO 10383, identifies the exchange not the security (e.g. XETR = Xetra, XLON = LSE).
RIC / ticker — vendor-specific, not globally unique.

The ISIN trap: AAPL (US0378331005) is listed on NASDAQ, Frankfurt, and dozens of other exchanges — one ISIN, many listings, many prices. Always pair an ISIN with a MIC or exchange code to get a unique price.
One security, many prices

The multi-listing problem

The same instrument (ISIN) can trade on 30+ exchanges simultaneously. Each listing has its own price, currency, trading hours, and liquidity.

When you query a market data API by ISIN without specifying a venue, you'll typically get multiple rows back — one per eligible listing. You need a strategy to pick one:

Primary venue — the "home" exchange defined in reference data (usually the exchange where the security was first listed).
Highest liquidity — pick the venue with the most trading volume over the past week or month.
Specific venue — pass a MIC code to get exactly one listing.

This is not specific to any vendor — it's a fundamental property of how global securities markets work.
Corporate actions — when reference data mutates

Events that change the meaning of historical prices

Corporate actions are events issued by a company that change its securities. They're the most common source of silent data bugs in financial systems.

Dividend: company pays cash per share. On the ex-date, the share price drops by the dividend amount. Historical prices before the ex-date need a downward adjustment to make charts continuous.
Stock split (e.g. 2-for-1): share count doubles, price halves. Every historical price before the split needs halving — otherwise your year-over-year return calculation will show a fake -50%.
Rights issue / spin-off: new shares issued, value redistributed. Complex adjustments needed.

Market data APIs typically offer two series: adjusted (suitable for charts and return calculations) and unadjusted (raw prices as they actually traded). Always know which one you're using.
02 — Equities

Stocks & Company Data

Ownership shares in a company. The most common instrument type in any market data feed. Price is set by continuous auction on an exchange. Equity data also includes fundamentals, key figures, and corporate actions.

Key Data Fields

Reference: isin name mic currency
Fundamentals: market_cap shares_outstanding pe_ratio eps
Dividends: dividend dividend_yield ex_date payment_date

Engineer Analogy

A stock is a shared mutable resource with a live price tag. The exchange is a pub/sub broker — market makers post bids/asks, trades are events emitted to all subscribers.

Corporate actions are like schema migrations — they change the meaning of all historical prices retroactively. A 2-for-1 split means every historical close needs halving. Adjusted vs unadjusted data = migrated vs original schema.

Settlement at T+2 means the trade is agreed today but cash and shares actually move two business days later. This matters for dividend entitlement — you must own the stock before ex-date at settlement.

Trading day timeline
04:00 ET — Pre-market
Quotes available but thin liquidity and wide spreads. No official open price yet. Pre-market prices are typically flagged separately in feeds.
09:30 ET — Opening auction
First official open price. High volatility spike and volume surge. The opening price is set by a call auction — all pending orders match simultaneously, not sequentially.
09:30–15:55 — Continuous trading
Bid/ask stream. Level 2 order book depth. Tick-by-tick trades available at this granularity. Aggregated into 1-min, 5-min, 15-min bars on request.
16:00 ET — Close
Official close price set by closing auction. EOD snapshot published. This is what gets stored as the daily bar in historical price series.
T+2 — Settlement
Cash and shares actually exchange two business days after the trade. Corporate action ex-dates are based on settlement date, not trade date — a common source of bugs.
Corporate actions

The tricky part — retroactive adjustment

Corporate actions change price or share count without a market trade. Every historical price API should offer both adjusted and unadjusted series.

Dividend: price drops by dividend amount on ex-date. Additive adjustment subtracts the dividend from all pre-ex-date prices so charts show continuous performance.
Stock split (2-for-1): price halved, shares doubled. Multiplicative adjustment halves all pre-split historical prices — otherwise a chart shows a fake -50% drop.
Always know which series you're using — mixing adjusted and unadjusted data in the same calculation produces wrong results silently.
03 — Fixed Income

Bonds & Notes

Debt instruments. The issuer borrows money, pays periodic coupons, then repays face value at maturity. Price moves inversely with interest rates. Bond data is complex because it trades OTC and comes from multiple data providers with partially overlapping coverage.

OTC / ExchangeYield-based Pricing Accrued InterestMulti-vendor PricingT+2 Settlement

Key Data Fields

Reference: isin issuer_name currency face_value
Structure: coupon_rate coupon_frequency maturity_date issue_date
Quote: clean_price dirty_price bid ask
Analytics: yield_to_maturity duration modified_duration
Accrual: accrued_interest day_count_convention
Credit: credit_rating rating_agency seniority

Engineer Analogy

A bond is a loan object with a payment schedule.

dirty_price = clean_price + accrued_interest

You quote clean but settle dirty. Market screens show clean price — the actual cash you hand over includes the accrued interest.

Duration is like a sensitivity coefficient — the ∂price/∂rate. A duration of 8 means a 1% rate rise drops the price ~8%. It's what "bonds go down when rates go up" actually means numerically.

Multi-provider reality: bond reference data comes from multiple vendors (e.g. WM, Bloomberg, EDI) with partially overlapping coverage. Your feed picks a source; different APIs may return different values for the same ISIN.

Bond lifecycle in the data feed
Issue date
Bond issued at par (100) or at discount/premium. ISIN assigned. Coupon rate and maturity date are fixed in reference data and never change during the bond's life.
Coupon dates
Periodic coupon payments (annual/semi-annual). Accrued interest resets to 0 after each payment. The dirty price drops by the coupon amount on payment date — looks like a price fall but isn't.
Daily
Clean price floats with market rates and credit conditions. Accrued interest ticks up every day (linearly between coupon dates). Yield to maturity recalculated from current price.
Maturity date
Face value repaid. Bond expires and moves to historical data. Your systems need to handle expired bonds — subscriptions, cache entries, and index compositions must be cleaned up.
04 — Foreign Exchange

Foreign Exchange — Cross Rates

The world's largest market (~$7.5T/day). Currency pairs trade 24/5. No central exchange — rates aggregated from interbank OTC network. The API delivers spot rates, cross rates, and fixing snapshots.

24/5 OTCSpot / Forward Pip-basedISO 4217ECB / WMR Fixings

Key Data Fields

Quote: bid ask mid last
Daily: open high low close
Reference: base_currency quote_currency pair
Derived: spread_pips pip_value

Cross rates — EUR/JPY is derived from EUR/USD × USD/JPY. Feeds may publish these as synthetic rates computed server-side.

Fixing rates: ECB fix at 16:00 CET, WMR at 16:00 London — point-in-time snapshots used as benchmarks for fund NAV and structured product settlement.

Engineer Analogy

FX is a global key-value store where keys are currency pairs and values update millions of times per day.

EUR/USD = 1.0850 means 1 EUR buys 1.0850 USD. Base currency is always the denominator of price.

The spread (bid/ask gap) is the market-maker's fee baked into the price — no separate commission.

Fixing rates (ECB 16:00 CET, WMR 16:00 London) are point-in-time snapshots used for fund NAV calculation and benchmark valuation.

FX data engineering nuances

No single official price — each liquidity provider quotes differently. The API aggregates multiple venues into a consolidated mid/bid/ask.

Timestamps are UTC. All request/response timestamps in the API are UTC. 16:00 London = 15:00 UTC (summer), 16:00 UTC (winter) — beware DST when computing fixing windows.

Weekend gaps: FX closes Friday ~17:00 ET and reopens Sunday ~17:00 ET. A week's worth of news can arrive in one price gap. Your historical chart service handles this as a gap in the time series — don't try to interpolate it.
05 — Derivatives

Derivatives — Options, Futures & Structured Products

Contracts whose value derives from an underlying asset. Multiple services handle different product types. All share the concept of an underlying, an expiry, and settlement mechanics.

OptionsFutures Warrants / TurbosCertificatesGreeksExpiry / Settlement

Key Data Fields

Options: underlying_isin strike expiry_date option_type (Call/Put)
Greeks: delta gamma theta vega rho
Volatility: implied_volatility historical_volatility
Quote: premium bid ask open_interest
Futures: contract_size tick_size last_trading_day settlement_type (cash/physical)
Leverage/Warrants: barrier leverage_ratio knock_out_level

Engineer Analogies

Option = a purchase order with an optional execution. You pay a premium for the right but not obligation.

Delta ≈ probability it expires in-the-money. Think of it as a confidence score (0→1).
Theta = time decay. Options lose value daily like a draining battery.

Future = a scheduled transaction. Both sides are obligated. Margin is the collateral held in escrow.

Turbo/Warrant = a leveraged bet with a kill switch (barrier). If the underlying touches the barrier at any tick, the product terminates immediately — even if price recovers.

Futures roll cycle — data feed impact
Contract listed
Front-month contract becomes active. Live quotes and building open interest appear in the feed. Open interest = total outstanding contracts; it's a measure of market participation.
~21 days before expiry
Roll period: volume migrates from front-month to next contract. Your feed shows both simultaneously. A continuous price series requires stitching — each vendor handles this differently.
Last trading day
Settlement price calculated (EDSP). Physical delivery notice (commodity futures) or cash settlement. The settlement_type field tells you which — it matters enormously for downstream systems.
Expiry
Contract expires. The next contract becomes front-month. A "roll gap" appears in any chart that wasn't adjusted for the roll — prices may jump even though nothing economically changed.
06 — Funds & ETFs

Funds, ETFs, ETCs & ETNs

Pooled investment vehicles. Mutual funds priced once daily at NAV. ETFs trade intraday. ETCs and ETNs are exchange-traded debt instruments backed by commodities or strategies. The fund service includes composition data (top holdings, countries, currencies).

NAV-basedAcc vs Dist ETF / ETC / ETNUCITSKIID / ESG

Key Data Fields

Valuation: nav nav_date currency
Classification: is_etf fund_type replication_method (physical/synthetic)
Costs: ter (total expense ratio) ongoing_charge
Size: aum (assets under management) shares_outstanding
Distribution: distribution_type (Acc/Dist) dividend_date dividend_amount
Composition: top holdings, country weights, sector weights, currency exposure

Engineer Analogy

A mutual fund is a daily batch job — NAV calculated once at market close, published T+1.

An ETF is the real-time streaming version — it has intraday bid/ask AND a once-daily NAV. The premium/discount (ETF price vs NAV) is like cache staleness — arbitrageurs (authorized participants) keep it near zero via a create/redeem mechanism.

ETCs are debt instruments backed by physical commodities. ETNs are debt instruments backed by a strategy return — both carry issuer credit risk unlike regular ETFs.

Identifier chaos — one fund, many IDs

Why fund identification is hard

One fund can have 50+ ISINs across share classes (currency hedged, accumulating, distributing, institutional, retail…). Common cross-jurisdictional identifiers in market data systems:

ISIN — global standard (but one fund ≠ one ISIN — each share class gets its own)
SEDOL — UK/Ireland
VALOR — Switzerland
WKN — Germany
Bloomberg ticker / Reuters RIC — vendor-specific, not globally unique

You need a mapping table. Vendors call this an instrument master or reference data service — it maps all identifiers to a canonical internal ID.
Fund data timeline
Intraday (ETFs only)
Bid/ask stream like equities. iNAV (indicative NAV) published every 15 seconds by the exchange — derived from real-time prices of underlying holdings.
16:00–18:00 local close
Fund administrator calculates official NAV. Applies FX fixing rates (typically WMR 16:00 London), corporate actions, and deducts TER. This is why fund NAVs use yesterday's FX rate.
T+1 morning
NAV published to data vendors. Subscription/redemption orders (placed yesterday) execute at this NAV. Market data feed delivers the updated nav and nav_date.
Distribution date
Distributing (Dist) class pays out income — NAV drops by distribution amount on ex-date. Accumulating (Acc) class reinvests — NAV grows without a drop.
07 — Quote & Time Series

Quote & Time Series Data

Three types of price data serve different engineering needs. Understanding their differences — and when to use each — is one of the most important practical skills in market data engineering.

Real-time / Delayed / EOD Tick-by-tickAggregation Periods Adjusted HistoryUTC timestamps

Snapshot Quotes

A snapshot of the current quote state. One request, one response. Three quality levels:

REALTIME — live, requires exchange entitlement
DELAYED — 15–20 min lag, cheaper entitlement
EOD — end-of-day close prices only

Typical fields: bid ask last_price volume turnover open high low close

Think of it as GET /current-state — stateless, pull-based.

Tick & Intraday Data

Every individual trade or quote update. Two modes:

Raw ticks — each event as it happens (trade, bid change, ask change)
Aggregated bars — OHLCV candlesticks computed over a time window

Common aggregation periods:
1 min 5 min 10 min 15 min 30 min 1 hour

Think of it as event sourcing — every state change recorded. Aggregated bars are materialized views over ticks.

Historical Price Series

End-of-day historical prices going back months or years. The key data-engineering concern here is corporate action adjustment.

Additive adjustment — subtracts past dividends from historical prices so charts appear continuous across ex-dates
Multiplicative adjustment — scales historical prices to account for stock splits
Adjustment source — which corporate action data provider to use (vendors differ; cross-check when accuracy matters)
Adjustment schema — simple backward adjustment (good for charts) vs. forward-adjusted (better for portfolio valuation)

When to use which type

Use Snapshot Quotes when

— Building a price display widget
— You need current bid/ask/last for a set of symbols
— You want a stateless, cacheable response
— You are not streaming (polling is fine)

Use Tick / Intraday Data when

— Building intraday charts
— You need OHLCV bars at specific intervals
— You need raw trades for volume analysis
— You need the exact time of each price move

Use Historical Series when

— Building long-term performance charts
— Computing returns, volatility, drawdown
— You need corporate-action adjusted series
— Backtesting strategies

UTC & Timezone Gotchas

All API timestamps are UTC. Exchange close times vary:
NYSE: 21:00 UTC (summer), 22:00 (winter)
LSE: 16:30 UTC (summer), 16:30 (winter)
Xetra: 17:30 UTC (summer), 16:30 (winter)

DST transitions in March/October cause one-hour shifts. Always store UTC, convert to local only for display.
